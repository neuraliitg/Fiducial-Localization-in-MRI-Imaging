The tricky domain of Medical Imaging

My experience in Inter IIT Tech Meet

The promising field of medical imaging requires you to be not just a Computer Vision engineer but a partial radiologist as well.

14 January, 2018

                                                Medical Imaging is one the most exciting fields that sits at the intersection of Neuroscience and Image Processing. In it's true sense, you have to be a bit radiologist as well as a good algorithmist, in order to write really efficient algorithms. There is a subtle difference between the conventional image processing and medical image processing. This difference stems from the way the images are produced in the different environment settings. As you all might be aware of the fact that image is a simply 2D matrix of pixel values(in case it's grayscale), image files for both of these applications varies accordingly.


Non-medical Images

JPEG Files are commonplace in digital still photography

The non-medical images are usually found in formats like *.JPEG,*.PNG,*.BMP,*.GIF. These non-medical formats are more concerned about keeping information about the image matrix. For example, JPEG file stores the information like height, width, no of componenets(Red, Green, Blue in case of color image) and pixel array values. Such data formats varies over the type of compression they provide and amount of detailing they can store inside the image.


Non-medical Images


Left: sMRI scan of a patient. Right : Diffusion Tensor Imaging(DTI)

On the other hand, medical images follow the popular convention of *.DICOM format. This is where the difference starts arising between a normal image and medical image. DICOM data format stores much more additional information than any other image data formats. Even it's storage depends upon the type of scanner you use. A GE(General Electric) MRI scanner might have more image contrast as compared to a CISCO scanner.





Here a DICOM image will contain data of only one section of slice out of the 3D volume that is to be generated by combining all these slices. The additional data present inside the DICOM file is solely for the purpose of developing 3D voume array of the patient's scan from different slices. These slices are pieces of a burger which are to be stacked in a particular fashion. Not arranging them in correct order will result in something which is not a burger.


DICOM Slicing Orientations


3 Different Image scan orientation

The DICOM images are taken in one of the 3 possible fashion :(a) Saggital (b) Corronal and (c) Axial. Arranging the slices taken in any of the above formats is one of the first part of getting things right. Usually, DICOM slices provided by most of the scanner are arranged in a way that their filename follows an ascending order of integers. The filenames will be like 16114.dcm,16115.dcm,16116.dcm etc. Here, it's not much of a trouble. But suppose that's not the case, then things goes bit twisted. You have to find the orientation from the DICOM Header and then coorelate it with the no. of the slice in that particular orientation. In Inter IIT Tech Meet, judges provided us with all orientations of scans in one folder and the total no files summed upto around 890. First, we assumed it to be a big MRI scan but then the pieces were redundant which led us to conclude that they are not all same orientations. Once we figured it out, we used the data stored in DICOM header to find out the orientation and then putting the slices correctly, according to their 'InstanceID' providied under same DICOM header.


As you can see, things are not straight forward as they might look. This part of the blog explains how to form the image out of the bundle of some DICOM files, but this tricky business is still not done. In the upcoming blog, I will post about how to actually develop the 3D scan of the patient. Hope you were not bored while reading it, as there is even much more to this amazing domain of Biomedical Engineering.
